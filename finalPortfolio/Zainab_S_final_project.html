Final Project Write Up - Brainamation Extension

My project presents an interactive, EEG-inspired visualization designed to illustrate

changes in brain state associated with attention and meditation. This is an extension/modification

of my original BrainImation project. Instead of displaying raw EEG waveforms, my project

maps EEG-derived features such as frequency band activity and cognitive state metrics onto

perceptual elements including motion, color, coherence, and spatial organization. This approach

emphasizes intuitive understanding of neural dynamics and allows brain states to be interpreted

qualitatively rather than through numerical signal plots.

Overall brain state is represented through the background environment and a dynamic

ocean-like wave pattern. Background color and brightness shift subtly in response to meditation

and alpha activity, creating a calm navy-green visual baseline that reflects global cortical arousal.

The ocean pattern encodes large-scale neural rhythms, where higher attention produces faster,

higher-amplitude, and more turbulent waves, while higher meditation results in slower and

smoother oscillations. These changes reflect well-established EEG associations between

attention, desynchronization, and low-frequency coherence during relaxed states.

Local neural dynamics are visualized using moving particles that represent distributed

neural populations. Particle motion is influenced by EEG frequency bands, with alpha activity

producing smooth, synchronized drift and beta activity introducing jitter and irregular motion.

Particle size increases with meditation and theta activity, reinforcing the link between slower

frequency bands and relaxed, internally oriented cognitive states. Functional connectivity is

illustrated through lines connecting nearby particles, which become more stable and widespread

during meditative states and fragment during high attentional demand, visually approximating

changes in coherence observed in EEG studies.

During periods of very high attention, brief explosion-like events occur (shown below),

representing transient bursts of heightened neural activity. These events dissipate quickly,

emphasizing that intense attentional states are dynamic and unstable rather than sustained. A

simplified dolphin figure moves through the visualization as a biological motion reference, with

swimming speed increasing during attention and movement becoming smoother and more

rhythmic during meditation. This element provides an intuitive, naturalistic anchor that helps

relate abstract neural states to familiar patterns of motion.

A persistent legend is included to support interpretation of the visualization. The legend

explicitly describes how changes in meditation, attention, and EEG frequency bands are mapped

onto visual features such as motion smoothness, noise, coherence, and turbulence. In addition,

the legend displays the dominant EEG frequency band in real time based on relative theta, alpha,

and beta activity. This reinforces the frequency-domain basis of EEG analysis and allows

viewers to directly link observed visual changes to underlying neural rhythms without requiring

prior technical expertise.

Together, these elements demonstrate how abstract visualization can be used to

communicate EEG-informed brain state dynamics in a way that is conceptually grounded,

interpretable, and visually engaging. By integrating global and local representations, frequency

band dominance, and explanatory legend cues, my project emphasizes understanding of brain

state transitions rather than raw signal measurement.
