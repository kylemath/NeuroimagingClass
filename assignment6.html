Assignment 6: Envisioning the Ideal Brain Imaging Technique

A research and writing assignment exploring what the "ideal" brain imaging technology might look like, what it would measure, and what barriers stand between current technology and that future.

Due Date: Monday, November 10, 2025 at 11:59 PM

Format: 3-5 page written report (PDF) ‚Ä¢ Choose ONE option (A, B, C, or D)

üéØ Assignment Overview

Throughout this course, we've explored the strengths and limitations of various brain imaging techniques ‚Äî from the millisecond precision of EEG but poor spatial resolution, to the excellent spatial detail of fMRI but slow hemodynamic responses, to the portability of fNIRS but limited depth penetration. Each method represents a compromise between competing priorities: temporal vs. spatial resolution, invasiveness vs. signal quality, cost vs. capability, portability vs. whole-brain coverage.

In this assignment, you'll step beyond current constraints and envision what an "ideal" brain imaging technique might look like. What would it measure? What physical principles would it exploit? What would the data look like? What engineering challenges would need to be solved? And most importantly: what aspects of brain function could we finally understand if we had this technology?

üß† Think Like a Neuroscientist-Engineer:

This isn't just a creative writing exercise ‚Äî it's an exercise in constrained innovation. Your proposed technique must be grounded in real physics, real biology, and real engineering principles. But it can push beyond what's currently feasible with clever solutions, emerging technologies, and forward thinking.

üìã Choose ONE Option (A, B, C, or D)

A

Design Your Ideal Brain Imaging Modality

The Challenge: Imagine you have unlimited research funding and access to cutting-edge technology from any field (photonics, quantum sensing, nanotechnology, machine learning, etc.). Design a brain imaging technique that solves problems current methods cannot.

Your Report Should Address:

1. What Would It Measure? (20%)

  • What aspect of neural activity would your technique detect?
    • Action potentials? Dendritic currents? Neurotransmitter release?
    • Blood flow? Metabolic activity? Oxygen consumption?
    • Structural changes? Protein dynamics? Gene expression?
    • Something completely new?
  • Why is this measurement meaningful? What would it tell us about brain function that we can't currently see?
  • What is the biological/physical basis for this signal?

2. What Would the Equipment Look Like? (25%)

  • What are the key hardware components?
    • Sensors/detectors (what type? where positioned?)
    • Energy source if needed (lasers? magnetic fields? ultrasound?)
    • Signal amplification/processing
    • Computational requirements
  • What physical principles does it exploit? (e.g., light scattering, magnetic resonance, acoustic propagation)
  • Is it invasive, minimally invasive, or completely non-invasive?
  • Is it portable/wearable or does it require a lab setup?
  • Estimated size, weight, power requirements?

3. What Would the Data Look Like? (20%)

  • Describe the raw signal ‚Äî what comes out of the sensors?
  • What is the temporal resolution? (microseconds? milliseconds? seconds?)
  • What is the spatial resolution? (whole brain? regional? single neuron?)
  • How much data per second? (KB? MB? GB?)
  • What kind of signal processing would be needed?
  • What would a typical "brain image" or visualization look like?

4. What Are the Limitations & Challenges? (20%)

  • What are the fundamental physical limitations?
    • Tissue scattering? Depth penetration? Signal-to-noise ratio?
    • Speed of light/sound constraints?
    • Quantum limits? Thermodynamic limits?
  • What are the practical engineering challenges?
    • Cost? Size? Power consumption?
    • Safety concerns? FDA approval barriers?
    • Computational requirements? Real-time processing?
  • What are the biological/medical constraints?
    • Does it require contrast agents? Genetic modification?
    • Can it be used repeatedly? In diverse populations?
    • Motion artifacts? Environmental interference?

5. What Would This Enable? (15%)

  • What neuroscience questions could finally be answered?
  • What clinical applications would become possible?
  • What types of brain-computer interfaces could be built?
  • Give 2-3 concrete examples of research or applications that would be transformed
üí° Examples to Inspire You:
  • Quantum magnetometry: Detecting single-neuron magnetic fields using diamond quantum sensors
  • Genetically-encoded voltage indicators: Fluorescent proteins that light up with action potentials
  • Ultrasound neuromodulation + imaging: Focused ultrasound that both stimulates and records
  • Photoacoustic tomography: Combining optical specificity with acoustic depth penetration
  • Implantable neuropixels arrays: Thousands of electrodes on flexible, biocompatible substrates
  • Thermal imaging: Detecting the minute temperature changes from neural metabolism
B

The "Perfect" Hybrid System ‚Äî Combining Existing Modalities

The Challenge: Instead of inventing something entirely new, design an optimal combination of 2-3 existing brain imaging techniques that complement each other's strengths and compensate for weaknesses. Explain how they would be integrated, what additional value the combination provides, and what engineering/analysis challenges arise from fusion.

Your Report Should Address:

1. Which Modalities and Why? (20%)

  • Choose 2-3 techniques from: EEG, MEG, fMRI, fNIRS, EROS, DCS, PET, OCT, two-photon microscopy, electrode arrays, etc.
  • Explain what each contributes to the hybrid system:
    • What information does each provide that the others don't?
    • How do their temporal/spatial resolutions complement each other?
    • What are their individual limitations?
  • Justify your specific combination ‚Äî why these techniques together? Why not others?

2. How Would They Be Integrated? (30%)

  • Physical integration:
    • Can the sensors/equipment physically coexist? (e.g., Can you do EEG inside an fMRI scanner?)
    • Do they interfere with each other? (magnetic fields affecting electronics, optical signals contaminating each other)
    • What engineering solutions would be needed to minimize interference?
  • Temporal integration:
    • Can they acquire data simultaneously? Or must they alternate?
    • How do you synchronize measurements across different time scales?
    • How do you align fast signals (EEG) with slow signals (fMRI)?
  • Spatial integration:
    • How do you co-register the spatial coordinate systems?
    • How do you fuse data at different spatial scales?
    • What anatomical reference frames would you use?

3. What Does Multimodal Data Look Like? (20%)

  • Describe what a researcher would see ‚Äî how would you visualize combined data?
  • What new metrics or features emerge from fusion that aren't in individual modalities?
  • How would you quantify the "added value" of the hybrid vs. individual techniques?
  • Give concrete examples: "When you see X in modality A and Y in modality B at the same time, that tells you Z about brain function"

4. Technical & Practical Challenges (20%)

  • What are the computational challenges? (data processing, storage, real-time fusion)
  • What are the cost implications? (feasibility for research labs, clinical use)
  • What are the practical workflow challenges? (participant burden, scan time, data analysis complexity)
  • Are there safety concerns from combining techniques?

5. Real-World Applications (10%)

  • What research questions require this multimodal approach?
  • What clinical scenarios would benefit from the hybrid system?
  • Give 2-3 specific examples where your hybrid would outperform individual modalities
üí° Existing Examples to Learn From:
  • EEG-fMRI: Used to localize epileptic seizures with both temporal precision (EEG) and spatial accuracy (fMRI)
  • PET-MRI: Combines metabolic information (PET) with structural detail (MRI) in cancer imaging
  • fNIRS-EEG: Portable combination tracking both hemodynamics and electrical activity
  • OCT-Doppler: Combines structural imaging with blood flow measurement in retinal imaging
  • Two-photon + electrophysiology: Optical imaging of neural activity combined with electrical recording
C

The Wearable Brain Monitor ‚Äî Engineering a Consumer-Ready Device

The Challenge: The "holy grail" of neurotechnology is a brain monitoring device as ubiquitous as a smartwatch ‚Äî something you wear all day that tracks your cognitive states, mental health, and brain performance. Design a realistic, consumer-ready wearable brain monitor that balances scientific validity with practical constraints (cost, comfort, battery life, ease of use).

Your Report Should Address:

1. Core Technology & Measurements (25%)

  • What sensing modality would you use?
    • Dry EEG electrodes? Optical (fNIRS/DCS)? Hybrid approach?
    • How many sensors? Where positioned on the head?
    • Why is this the best choice for a consumer device?
  • What metrics would it report to users?
    • Focus/attention? Stress/relaxation? Sleep quality? Cognitive load?
    • How would these metrics be derived from raw signals?
    • What's the scientific evidence these metrics are valid?

2. Hardware Design & Form Factor (25%)

  • Physical design:
    • Headband? Earbuds? Eyeglasses? Behind-the-ear device? Other?
    • Weight target? Materials? Adjustability for different head sizes?
    • How comfortable for all-day wear?
  • Technical specifications:
    • Battery life target? (8 hours? 24 hours?)
    • Wireless connectivity? (Bluetooth? WiFi?)
    • Onboard processing vs. phone-based processing?
    • Water resistance? Durability?
  • Signal quality vs. convenience tradeoffs:
    • How do you ensure good signal despite hair, movement, sweat?
    • What compromises in signal quality are acceptable for convenience?

3. User Experience & Software (20%)

  • Smartphone app interface:
    • What does the user see? (real-time graphs? simple scores? visualizations?)
    • How often do they get feedback? (continuous? hourly summaries? daily reports?)
    • What insights/recommendations does the AI provide?
  • Use cases & scenarios:
    • Meditation/mindfulness training? Productivity optimization? Sleep tracking?
    • Mental health monitoring? Cognitive decline detection?
    • What is the "killer app" that makes people want to wear it every day?

4. Validation & Scientific Credibility (15%)

  • How would you validate that your metrics are meaningful?
    • What studies would you run?
    • What correlations with behavior/performance would you need to demonstrate?
    • How do you avoid the "junk science" trap of existing consumer brain devices?
  • What is your strategy for regulatory approval (if seeking medical claims)?
  • How do you communicate uncertainty and limitations to consumers?

5. Business & Market Reality (15%)

  • Cost analysis:
    • Estimated manufacturing cost? Retail price target?
    • How do you reach the <$200 price point of smartwatches?
  • Market positioning:
    • Who is your target user? (wellness enthusiasts? productivity seekers? patients?)
    • Who are your competitors? (Muse? Kernel? Neurosity? Others?)
    • What makes your device better?
  • Privacy & ethics:
    • How is brain data stored? Who has access?
    • What are the ethical concerns with continuous brain monitoring?
    • How do you prevent misuse (e.g., employers requiring brain monitoring)?
üí° Real Devices to Learn From:
  • Muse S (Gen 2): 4-channel EEG headband for meditation, ~$250, 10hr battery
  • Muse S Athena (2025): First consumer EEG+fNIRS hybrid, tracks mental effort and blood flow
  • Kernel Flow: Time-domain fNIRS, 52 channels, lab-quality data in wearable form
  • Neurosity Crown: 8-channel EEG, focuses on productivity/focus monitoring
  • NextMind (discontinued): Visual cortex monitor for controlling interfaces with attention
  • OpenBCI: Open-source EEG, customizable but not consumer-friendly
D

Portabilizing Lab Technology ‚Äî Bringing Research Tools to the Real World

The Challenge: Many powerful neuroimaging techniques are confined to laboratories because they require large, expensive, power-hungry equipment. Choose one "lab-bound" technology and design a portable/wearable version that maintains scientific validity while becoming practical for real-world use.

Choose a Technology to Portabilize:

Examples of lab-bound technologies:
  • MEG (Magnetoencephalography): Requires liquid helium cooling, magnetically shielded room, massive sensors
  • High-density EEG: 128-256 channels with gel/saline, long setup time, tethered to amplifiers
  • Two-photon microscopy: Requires large lasers, precise optics, head-fixed animals
  • PET scanning: Radioactive tracers, massive detectors, medical facility required
  • High-field fMRI: 3T-7T magnets, cannot move during scan, claustrophobic bore
  • Transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS): Heavy coil, precise positioning, tethered to power supply
  • Patch-clamp electrophysiology: Single-cell recording requiring micropipettes and stable mounting

Your Report Should Address:

1. Why This Technology Matters (15%)

  • What does the lab version measure that's scientifically valuable?
  • What discoveries or applications has it enabled?
  • Why would a portable version be transformative?
  • What research or clinical applications currently can't happen because it's lab-bound?

2. What Makes It Lab-Bound? (20%)

  • Size/weight constraints: What components are large and heavy?
  • Power requirements: How much power does it need? Why?
  • Environmental requirements: Shielding? Temperature control? Vibration isolation?
  • Setup complexity: How long does prep take? What expertise required?
  • Cost barriers: What makes it expensive? ($10K? $100K? $1M+?)
  • Rank these barriers ‚Äî which is the biggest obstacle to portability?

3. Your Portable Design (30%)

  • Core innovation: What's your key insight for making it portable?
    • New sensor technology? Miniaturized components? Different physics?
    • Computational tricks to compensate for reduced hardware?
    • Clever mechanical design?
  • Form factor:
    • Wearable? Portable cart? Briefcase-sized? Car-sized?
    • Weight target? Power requirements (battery life)?
    • Setup time: <5 min? <30 min?
  • Technical specifications:
    • What signal quality metrics are maintained vs. compromised?
    • Quantify the tradeoffs (e.g., "75% of lab sensitivity but 1/100th the size")
    • What's "good enough" for real-world use?

4. Engineering Challenges & Solutions (20%)

  • What are the 3 biggest technical challenges in your design?
  • For each challenge, propose a specific solution (even if speculative)
  • What recent technological advances make your design feasible now (or soon)?
    • New materials? Better batteries? Cheaper sensors?
    • AI/ML to compensate for hardware limitations?
    • Manufacturing techniques? (3D printing, flexible electronics, etc.)

5. Impact & Feasibility (15%)

  • New applications enabled: What can researchers/clinicians now do that they couldn't before?
  • Timeline to reality: Is this feasible in 2 years? 5 years? 10 years? Why?
  • Cost projection: Estimated cost for portable version vs. lab version?
  • Market size: Who would buy/use this? (research labs? hospitals? consumers?)
  • Validation strategy: How would you prove the portable version gives equivalent data to lab version?
üí° Real-World Examples of Successful Portabilization:
  • MEG ‚Üí OPM-MEG: Optically-pumped magnetometers replace SQUIDs, eliminating liquid helium and enabling wearable MEG
  • Lab fNIRS ‚Üí Wearable fNIRS: LEDs replace fiber-coupled lasers, making <$5K systems vs. $100K+ lab systems
  • Bench EEG ‚Üí Dry EEG: Dry electrodes eliminate gel prep, enabling consumer headbands
  • Hospital ultrasound ‚Üí Butterfly iQ: Chip-based ultrasound (not piezo crystals) creates phone-sized device
  • Lab microscopes ‚Üí Foldscope: Origami paper microscope costs <$1, used for field diagnostics
  • CT scanner ‚Üí Portable CT: Reduced to suitcase-sized for military/emergency use

‚ö†Ô∏è Critical: Verify Everything!

AI tools frequently "hallucinate" ‚Äî they make up facts, citations, and links that sound real but aren't!

  • Check every citation: AI often invents paper titles, authors, and DOIs that don't exist
    • Use Google Scholar or PubMed to verify papers actually exist
    • Actually read (or at least skim) the papers you cite
    • Click every link to ensure it works
  • Verify technical claims: AI confidently states incorrect physics or biology

    • If something seems too good to be true, it probably is
  • Check numbers: AI makes up statistics, costs, and specifications
    • Find actual specifications from manufacturer websites or papers
    • Sanity-check: Does "1000x improvement" really make sense?
  • Ensure coherence: AI sometimes contradicts itself within the same document
