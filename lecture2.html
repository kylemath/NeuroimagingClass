Opening: The EEG Signal Chain (20 minutes)

Key Question: How do we extract meaningful information from noisy brain signals?

EEG signal path: signals originate from synchronized postsynaptic currents in pyramidal neurons, summating into mesoscopic fields that reach the scalp. Front‚Äëend hardware couples these microvolt‚Äëscale potentials through contact electrodes into differential, high‚Äëimpedance instrumentation amplifiers (e.g., OpenBCI front‚Äëends on TI ADS1299). The amplified analog signal is anti‚Äëaliased, digitized at an appropriate sampling rate with stable clocking, and passed to software where filters, artifact models, and spectral/temporal analyses transform raw voltages into interpretable features.

Live demo: connecting a Muse headset reveals unprocessed traces combining neural activity with ocular, myogenic, and mains interference. EEG Fundamentals: What We're Actually Measuring (45 minutes)

The Physics of EEG

EEG reflects the summed postsynaptic activity of large populations of pyramidal neurons rather than individual action potentials, which are too brief and small at the scalp. Signals spread through tissue, giving EEG outstanding millisecond temporal precision but comparatively coarse spatial resolution on the order of centimeters.

Electrode Placement and the 10-20 System

The international 10‚Äì20 system standardizes placement using anatomical landmarks and percentage spacing so that locations like Cz are comparable across labs and participants (10‚Äì20 overview). Mechanistically, electrode quality depends on contact impedance (skin prep, gel/dry sensor design), reference montage (linked mastoids, average reference, Laplacian), and cable/sensor shielding to limit common‚Äëmode noise. A brief demonstration can show measuring nasion‚Äìinion and ear‚Äëto‚Äëear distances, estimating 10%/20% positions, and discussing trade‚Äëoffs between gel‚Äëbased and dry electrodes.

Signal Processing Fundamentals (50 minutes)

Filtering: Separating Signal from Noise

EEG spans roughly 0.1‚Äì100+ Hz. High‚Äëpass filtering suppresses slow drifts and DC offsets (capacitive skin effects, electrode polarization), low‚Äëpass filtering reduces high‚Äëfrequency noise and myogenic activity, and band‚Äëpass filtering isolates bands (e.g., alpha, beta) for targeted analysis. Event-Related Potentials (ERPs) and Experimental Design (40 minutes)

Time-Locked Brain Responses

ERPs are ensemble averages of time‚Äëlocked cortical responses. The averaging operation increases SNR proportional to the square root of the number of trials under an assumption of phase‚Äëlocked signal and zero‚Äëmean noise. Component morphology reflects overlapping neural generators convolved with the system‚Äôs temporal filtering; careful baseline correction, artifact censoring, and filter choices (e.g., avoiding excessive high‚Äëpass) are critical to avoid distorting latency and amplitude. For fundamentals and best practice, see Luck‚Äôs ERP textbook.

Trigger Systems and Synchronization

Accurate ERP work hinges on precise timing. Hardware triggers (TTL) align event onsets to the EEG sampling clock with microsecond‚Äëscale jitter; software triggers depend on OS scheduling and graphics pipelines and require drift checks. Robust stacks implement monotonic time bases, clock drift estimation, and periodic sync pulses (photodiode for visual onset) to validate actual stimulus timing. See Psychtoolbox, PsychoPy timing, jsPsych, and LabStreamingLayer. In a visual oddball, well‚Äëformed P300s depend as much on verified timing and clean preprocessing as on paradigm design.
