===========================================
EXPERIMENTAL RATIONALE
===========================================

Some controversial studies have claimed that neural activity might correlate 
with ultra-weak biophoton emission detectable at the skin surface. The proposed 
mechanisms include:
- Oxidative stress producing chemiluminescence
- Lipid peroxidation during intense neural activity
- Quantum effects in microtubules (highly speculative!)

This experiment tests whether mental effort (mental arithmetic, visualization) 
produces a measurable change in photon detection at the forehead compared to 
relaxed baseline states.

üî¨ Critical Experimental Design Principle:
The experiment is designed with PROPER CONTROLS to test alternative explanations:
- Temperature changes from blood flow?
- Movement artifacts?  
- Light leaks into the dark room?
- Pure chance / statistical noise?

===========================================
APPARATUS SETUP
===========================================

Photon Detection System:
- Device: Hamamatsu H10722-110 photon counting module
- Quantum Efficiency: ~10% at 500-600nm (peak sensitivity)
- Dark Count Rate: ~35-50 counts/second at room temperature
- Detection Area: 8mm diameter active area
- Temperature: Passively cooled (not chilled, room temp ~22¬∞C)

Positioning:
- Detector placed 5 cm from subject's forehead (frontal cortex region)
- Fixed mount to prevent movement artifacts
- Black fabric hood to eliminate external light
- Subject's eyes either open or closed (varied by condition)

Dark Room Environment:
- Light-sealed chamber with blackout curtains
- Verified complete darkness with IR camera (no visible/IR light sources)
- Door sealed with black tape
- Subject allowed 10 min dark adaptation before each session
- Temperature monitored: 21-22¬∞C throughout all recordings

Data Acquisition:
- Photon counts integrated over 100ms time bins (10 Hz sampling)
- Each recording: 5 minutes = 300 seconds = 3000 data points
- Timestamp precision: 1 ms

===========================================
STATISTICAL CHARACTERISTICS
===========================================

Photon Count Distribution:
- Follows Poisson statistics (expected for photon counting)
- Mean count per 100ms bin: ~4.2 counts
- Equivalent to ~42 photons/second
- Standard deviation: ~2.1 counts (sqrt of mean for Poisson)

Effect Sizes (compared to control_no_person baseline):
- Baseline (eyes open/closed): +0.5-0.8% (negligible)
- Meditation: +0.4% (negligible)  
- Visualization: +2.8% (subtle)
- Mental Math #1: +3.5% (subtle but potentially detectable)
- Mental Math #2: +3.2% (replication)

Statistical Significance:
Given Poisson noise, 3.5% effect with 3000 samples:
- Expected p-value: ~0.02-0.05 (borderline significant with proper analysis)
- Requires careful windowing and averaging to detect
- Bootstrapping or permutation tests recommended

===========================================
ALTERNATIVE EXPLANATIONS TO CONSIDER
===========================================

‚ö†Ô∏è Students MUST address these in their analysis:

1. Temperature Drift:
   - PMT dark current increases ~7% per ¬∞C
   - Room temp varied ¬±0.5¬∞C over 2-hour session
   - Could this explain the effect?

2. Movement Artifacts:
   - Did subject move more during mental tasks?
   - Could vibrations from sub-vocal counting affect detector?

3. Light Leaks:
   - Could mental effort cause changes in body position that let in light?
   - Control recordings show good darkness, but double-check

4. Order Effects:
   - Later recordings have higher counts ‚Äî pure time effect?
   - Temperature accumulation over session?

5. Statistical Noise:
   - With enough comparisons, some will be "significant" by chance
   - Multiple comparison correction needed?

6. Confirmation Bias:
   - Experimenter (instructor) knows the hypothesis
   - Could this affect which trials are "acceptable"?

===========================================
ANALYSIS GUIDANCE FOR STUDENTS
===========================================

Required Steps:

1. Visualize Raw Data:
   - Plot photon counts over time for each condition
   - Calculate mean and standard error for each condition
   - Compare to control (no person) baseline

2. Statistical Testing:
   - Perform t-tests comparing each condition to control
   - Use bootstrap or permutation tests for robust p-values
   - Correct for multiple comparisons (Bonferroni or FDR)

3. Time Window Analysis:
   - Average counts in 30-second windows
   - Does the effect persist throughout the 5-minute recording?
   - Or is it only at the beginning/end?

4. Control for Temperature Drift:
   - Fit linear trend to control_no_person data
   - Subtract this drift from all other conditions
   - Does the effect remain?

5. Effect Size Estimation:
   - Calculate Cohen's d or percent change from baseline
   - Is the effect physiologically plausible?
   - Compare to published biophoton emission studies

Critical Discussion Points:

‚úì Is the effect statistically significant?
‚úì Is it replicable? (compare two mental math sessions)
‚úì Can we rule out temperature drift?  
‚úì Can we rule out movement artifacts?
‚úì What additional controls would strengthen the conclusion?
‚úì Is the effect size consistent with any known biophysical mechanism?

üéØ The Point: Teach rigorous experimental design and skeptical data analysis!
